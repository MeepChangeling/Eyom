Classifications of Habitable Planets

SEIO Institute of Astrobiology

The SEIO Institute of Astrobiology has developed a detailed classification system for habitable planets based on a range of environmental factors. This system is intended to assist in the identification and study of planets that support conditions conducive to the survival of sapient life forms. The classification system uses an alphanumeric code, summarizing key planetary characteristics to provide a quick reference guide for planetary conditions. This article outlines the primary classifications, focusing on temperature, atmospheric composition, gravity, hydrosphere, and surface stability.

Summary Code System

Each planet can be summarized by a code combining these classes, e.g., T3-A2-G3-H4-S3 would describe a temperate planet with moderate oxygen, standard gravity, abundant surface water, and stable surface conditions.

1. Temperature Class (T)

The temperature of a planet is critical for maintaining the chemical reactions necessary for life. The following subclasses define the temperature range:

  • T1: Frigid (< -50°C)
  • T2: Cold (-50°C to 0°C)
  • T3: Temperate (0°C to 30°C)
  • T4: Warm (30°C to 50°C)
  • T5: Hot (> 50°C)

2. Atmospheric Composition Class (A)

The atmosphere's chemical composition must support respiration and protect from harmful radiation. This classification considers the presence and concentration of key gases:

  • A1: Rich in oxygen (>20% O2)
  • A2: Moderate oxygen (10-20% O2)
  • A3: Low oxygen (<10% O2)
  • A4: High carbon dioxide (CO2 > 5%)
  • A5: Toxic or unbreathable (high levels of harmful gases)

3. Gravity Class (G)

The planet’s gravity affects the physiological functions of sapient species, particularly regarding movement and bodily functions. Gravity classifications are as follows:

  • G1: Microgravity (<0.3g)
  • G2: Low gravity (0.3g to 0.8g)
  • G3: Standard gravity (0.8g to 1.2g)
  • G4: High gravity (1.2g to 1.5g)
  • G5: Extreme gravity (>1.5g)

4. Hydrosphere Class (H)

Water availability is a key factor for sustaining life. The hydrosphere classification describes the abundance and state of liquid water:

  • H1: No water (dry)
  • H2: Trace water (minimal, possibly underground)
  • H3: Limited surface water (rivers, lakes, small seas)
  • H4: Abundant surface water (oceans covering 30-70% of the surface)
  • H5: Water world (>70% of the surface covered by water)

5. Surface Stability Class (S)

The surface stability of a planet, including tectonic activity, weather patterns, and surface composition, impacts long-term habitability:

  • S1: Volatile (frequent tectonic activity, severe weather)
  • S2:Active (moderate tectonic activity, varied weather)
  • S3: Stable (low tectonic activity, mild weather)
  • S4: Calm (minimal tectonic activity, stable climate)
  • S5: Quiescent (geologically dead, no tectonic activity)

This classification system is a fundamental tool for cataloging and comparing habitable planets. The use of a concise alphanumeric code allows for easy reference and communication within the scientific community, streamlining the search for and study of planets that may support sapient life forms.
