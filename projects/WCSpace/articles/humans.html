Appearance

Humans (GTCS: Sapientia bipedia terrans bipedae dextera oculata Homo sapiens) are a fairly plain looking species. They are descended from a species of QeH, best described as a member of the ghIlab naHmey classification. Their evolution is similar to the Gid, baring of course the Gid's descent from a species of Huch rather than QeH (Both species categories are exceptionally similar and easily confused.). While their appearance is more varied than the Gid, they are still quite limited in morphology and coloration.

Human coloration and morphology depends entirely on their ethnic group, of which there are many. Humans currently recognise one thousand sixty five1065 ethnic groups, but there are far fewer than one thousand sixty five1065 unique appearance sets for these groupings. Unfortunately humans are extremely sensitive to ethnicity, an unfortunate taboo for those wishing to study their species. They can become extremely defensive or even aggressive if asked innocuous questions such as "Why do you call yourself black when you're brown?" or "I'm sorry, I can't see any difference between you. Can you please explain?". This trait is common to primitive species, and makes determining the standard human appearance far more difficult than it needs to be.

Humans have a social concept called "race", an identity created, often by socially dominant groups, to establish meaning in a social context. Different cultures define different racial groups, often focused on the largest groups of social relevance, and these definitions can change over time. This is the closest humans get to being willing to average their morphology and coloration into a standard appearance. Currently the races recognised by Earth's dominant power are:

  • American Indian
  • Alaska Native
  • Asian
  • Black / African American
  • Native Hawaiian
  • Pacific Islander
  • White / Caucasian

Despite two of these categories having colors in their names, it is considered immoral and unethical in most human cultures to define people by the color of their skin. Several wars in their recent history have involved genocide based on racial identities. Consequently describing any of these groups in detail might be considered offensive enough to cause hostilities, and therefore will be skipped. We apologize for the inconvenience.

Our only recommendation is to view human media until you pick up on the differences personally, or to talk with those who have.

Mental Abilities

Humans are currently presumed prefect in terms of adapting to and utilizing technology. Their score of twenty20 could potentially be a nineteen19 once more individuals take the test, but this seems unlikely. In either case, their ability to utilize technology and adapt to technology is either prefect, or nearly prefect.

In laymen's terms, this means humans are capable of truly insane technological feats for their development level (Cee FourC4). Examples include:

  • Pre-nanomachine vaccinations and immunizations.
  • Silicone computer chips with a mere five5nm transistor size.
  • Landed on their moon and both neighboring planets using combustion powered spacecraft. A galactic first.
  • Adapting a natural bacteria's replication method to create a customizable (if crude) gene editing tool. A random human unrelated to their academic institutions made use of it to cure a genetic condition, by their on will and machinations.
    • This occured less than seventy years after they discovered DNA exists, and within 25 years of mapping their genome.
  • Created several space stations. Using only combustion powered space craft. By hand. In space. Without so much as a hardsuit.
  • Created a rotary prop aircraft which functioned for several years on Sol IV.
  • Drove several diseases to extinction. Intentionally.
  • Created optical telescopes capable of taking images of objects hundreds of lightyears away.
  • Developed a functional global digital communications system before artificial intelligence. The system operates using an elaborate series of rules and an incomprehensible web of standards, yet almost never fails.
  • Have created multiple atomicly flat structures without the aid of force manipulator technology.
  • Fully mapped their species genome in only thirteen13 years using computers most of us would assume couldn't so much as display text.
  • Have managed to create a crude approximation of AI by using basic machine learning to learn to imitate an aspect of sapience, and linking them together for added functionality.

All of these achievements are the result of humans ability to take seemingly unrelated technologies and apply them to new situations or with other technologies. They can use this simple principal to overcome a frankly ridiculous number of challenges. One need only look at how their primitive graphics systems handles lighting and shadows to see just how ridiculous and backwards humans can be... and yet their ideas work more often than not.

In addition to their potential perfection in terms of technological adaptation, their elevated scores in creative thinking, pattern recognition and analytical thinking allow humans to preform a feat of mental mastery which is the key behind everything else they have done. A group of humans (usually five to ten5-10) with similar interests can collaborate on a project and achieve a level of progress comparable to Class III AI systems.

A group of humans determined to solve a problem will almost certainly find a way to do so, provided they have the funding to preform their work. This ability has been endemic to their species since their paleolithic era. It is the reason why it took them a mere hundred years (by their calendar) to finish their industrial revolution. It is why it only took them sixty years to go from their first powered aircraft to landing on their moon. It is why if given middle school physics and engineering text books we expect humans could quickly solve any small to medium scale problem on any of our worlds.

Even their least educated and poorest individuals can and do make use of this ability, often to return complex equipment they do not fully understand to a working state using only scraps which happened to be at hand. At the highest levels, humans were recently able to create a vaccine for a brand new virus which had become a pandemic in less than a year. Under no circumstances should anyone underestimate the human ability to collaborate. It truly cannot be described in mere words.

Mentality

The most startling ability within the human species is their unique capacity for self-delusion. A human can convince itself it will succeed at something, or is in the right so completely they often find a way to win if the odds are not truly impossible. The downside being many humans cannot conceive of something being truly impossible and develop grudges or worldviews based on perceived mistreatment.

Humans are irrational. They act against each other's interests on every scale from interpersonal to international. They compete viciously and brutally (often through total war) for limited resources even if they do not need those resources. Their mere existence as limited can be enough to a human to warrant doing anything to possess them. This is due to humans high capacity for asperation, which is often driven by greed yes, but just as often by a sense of kinship.

Combine this with their species sense of compassion and ridiculous tolerance of adversity, and its little wonder why they act as if they can make it through any hardship for so long and so hard that they managed to actually find a way to do so. In simpler terms, humanity has weaponized delusion as a survival tool.

While most species within the galaxy have similar ideas of recreational activities (sports and the arts being seemingly universal), humans enjoy taking things to extremes. Human recreation includes and often emphasizes physical violence, intense mental competition, deliberate self poisoning, and in many cases deliberately placing themselves into lethal situations.Remember, humans current solution for space travel is placing their fragile bodies in a thin metal cylinder and flinging it into space using a controlled explosion. And it works.

While this could describe any primitive species at some point in its history, these traits have not only remained as humans have begun to globalize, but have expanded. A popular human sport is called base jumping, it involves scaling a tall object or suitable natural land feature and jumping off of it with only a thin cloth membrane to halt one's fall. Many humans have preserved their ancient martial arts not merely out of scholarly interest and cultural duty, but to make use of them in sporting competitions.

Even intellectually inclined humans appear to greatly enjoy danger as they have developed hybrid sports such as Chess Boxing (alternating rounds of a complex strategy game and brutal physical combat emphasizing blows to the head), amature rocketry (essentially the design and manufacture of explosives to send objects as high as possible), and competing for various records which require decades of expertise and dedicated teams... all to attempt to break the sound barrier in a wheeled vehicle.

Humans are amazingly self aware of this aspect of themselves. Their modern culture has created an entire category of jokes based on the idea of alien life meeting them and finding much of their behavior to be "batshit insane". They call it "Humans are Space Orcs" and little do they know, it's essentially correct. This genera of humor also includes observations on their self-contradictory behaviors and is a remarkable example of their mental abilities mixing with their philosophy and sense of self.

Religion

Humans are on average religious extremists (by galactic standards. By their own standards, only a small number of individuals are religious extremists. We could count those individuals as hyper extremists.). This is understandable as Earth happens to be a Class IX Shrine World, with a resident population of extradimentional entities of Class III or higher numbering in the low tens of thousands humans were bound to develop strong religious beliefs and practices.

Interestingly despite Earth's status, humans are relatively insensitive to dimensional flux and have a low psionic potential. The reasons for this are unknown.

Religion has shaped human history in ways both constructive and destructive. On the positive side, religious institutions have often been the bedrock of community cohesion, offering social support, ethical guidelines, and a sense of belonging. They have inspired humans to create some of their most enduring works of art, literature, and architecture. The concepts of charity, justice, and human dignity often have roots in religious teachings, influencing laws and societal norms.

Moreover, religion has been a catalyst for scientific and philosophical inquiry. Many of humanity's early scholars were also religious figures who sought to understand the divine by exploring the natural world. The drive to comprehend the universe and humanity's place within it has propelled human innovation and led to discoveries that have benefitted all sentient life forms.

However, the influence of religion is not without its darker aspects. Throughout their history, humans have committed atrocities in the name of religion. Wars, persecution, and cultural destruction have often been justified by religious ideologies. These conflicts, while sometimes politically motivated, are frequently couched in religious terms, leading to long-standing divisions and suffering. Occasionally even permanent incompatibility between groups can arise from religious differences as simple and ancient as a succession dispute nearly one and a half millennia old. Religion has also been used to justify social inequalities, stifling progress and maintaining oppressive systems under the guise of divine will.

Wether or not these events are purely inventions of the human mind, or genuine orders from extra dimensional entities is a coin toss. Various clerics and monks have attempted to contact Earth's higher dimensions, but have been either unsuccessful, or only able to reach Class I or II entities, who lack the power necessary to matter enough to their society to have the answers we seek.

Environs Typically Inhabited

We have obtained permission to use sections of "A Study of Homo sapiens' Survival Mechanisms in Varied Environments" by Doctor Tarum for this section. We believe his perspective illustrates the point far better than ours ever could.

"Humans are an intriguing case study in survival. Unlike many species that are specialized to thrive in specific environments, humans exhibit a generalist approach. They lack the fur of polar animals, the water retention abilities of desert creatures, and the wings or gills of other planetary fauna. Yet, through a combination of biological plasticity, cultural innovation, and sheer willpower, they have managed to colonize nearly every terrestrial environment on their planet."

"Humans are primarily a tropical species, and their evolutionary roots in warmer climates are evident in their physiological responses to heat. The most significant adaptation is their ability to sweat profusely, a process known as evaporative cooling. This mechanism allows humans to regulate their core temperature even in environments where ambient temperatures exceed their normal body temperature (approximately 37°C). However, this adaptation comes at a cost: dehydration. In high heat environments, a human can lose several liters of water per day through sweat, necessitating a constant intake of fluids to avoid heatstroke and death."

"Inhabitants of arid regions such as the Sahara and the Arabian Peninsula have developed cultural practices that mitigate the effects of extreme heat. Clothing in these regions is typically loose and covers much of the body, reducing direct exposure to the sun while allowing air circulation to facilitate evaporative cooling. Furthermore, the timing of daily activities is often adjusted, with work and travel occurring during the cooler morning and evening hours, and rest taken during the midday heat.

The Bedouins, nomadic inhabitants of the Arabian deserts, exemplify human adaptation to extreme heat. Their knowledge of water sources, both permanent and seasonal, coupled with their use of camels, which can go without water for several days, allows them to traverse vast, inhospitable landscapes. Their diet, rich in dates and milk, provides the necessary nutrients to endure the harsh desert climate."

"In stark contrast to their ability to endure heat, humans possess only minimal physiological adaptations to cold. These include the constriction of blood vessels in the extremities to preserve core body heat, and shivering to generate warmth through muscle activity. Prolonged exposure to cold, however, can lead to frostbite and hypothermia, conditions that are often fatal if untreated."

"Survival in cold environments is heavily reliant on cultural adaptations. Indigenous peoples of the Arctic, such as the Inuit, have developed sophisticated clothing systems utilizing animal furs and skins, which provide excellent insulation. Additionally, their traditional diets are high in fat, which is metabolized to produce heat in the body. Shelter is also critical: the construction of igloos, which trap body heat, and the use of communal sleeping arrangements help conserve warmth during the long polar nights.

The Inuit people’s lifestyle is a masterclass in cold weather survival. Their knowledge of the environment allows them to hunt and fish in conditions that would incapacitate less adapted humans. The consumption of raw, fatty foods like seal blubber not only provides necessary calories but also supplies vital nutrients that are scarce in the Arctic, such as Vitamin C."

"High altitudes pose a significant challenge due to the reduced oxygen levels in the atmosphere. Humans respond to this by increasing their breathing rate and heart rate, which temporarily boosts oxygen intake. Over time, the body produces more red blood cells, improving oxygen transport. However, these adaptations are not without risk. Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS), High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE), and High Altitude Cerebral Edema (HACE) are serious, often fatal, conditions that can occur if the body fails to adapt adequately.

The Sherpas, renowned for their mountaineering skills, have adapted to the extreme altitudes of the Himalayas, where oxygen levels are as low as 50% of those at sea level. They exhibit a unique genetic adaptation—a variant of the EPAS1 gene—that allows for more efficient use of oxygen. This, combined with their deep cultural knowledge of the mountains, allows them to survive at the highest elevation on their planet without much difficulty."

"Humans have limited natural resistance to radiation. However, their ability to understand and manipulate their environment has allowed them to mitigate the effects of radiation exposure. This is particularly evident in their use of protective clothing, lead shielding, and the development of technologies that monitor radiation levels.

The human ability to maximize their technological advantage and use collective lateral thinking in their problem solving has allowed them to have an excellent survival rate even in the most irradiated conditions available to them. On Earth the undisputed most irradiated zone is the ruins of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant near the city of Pripyat, Ukraine. The reactor, a primitive fission system, underwent a full meltdown due to corruption reducing its safety mechanisms and improper construction. At the height of the disaster, the radiation levels were estimated to be three hundred300 Sieverts per hour, not quite instantly fatal to a human, but enough to kill them within the month. Currently the radiation has subsided to 2.5 microsieverts per hour.

Context thus established, the Chernobyl disaster response saw the deployment of over 600,000 workers, known as "liquidators," who undertook the dangerous task of cleaning up radioactive material. The overwhelming majority survived due to the use of protective gear and the careful management of exposure times. Only ten percent10% of liquidators died from radiation related conditions."

"Humans are remarkably adept at surviving in all manner of conditions. They have a term for planets like Earth within their fiction. Death Worlds. Planets which are inhabitable, but only just, and teaming with preditors of all kinds. They see themselves as the undisputed kings of a Death World, a view that I am inclined to agree with. Biologically humans have many shortcomings in terms of environmental adaptation, but they are driven to live wherever they can carve out a niche for themselves. With the right technology and a few clever ideas there is nowhere humans are not capable of surviving and few places they could not thrive.

Once humans take to the stars they will seek to settle far off exotic worlds. Wanderlust is in their blood. Fortunately for the galaxy, the humans who seek to live in remote places, frontiers, and/or rural places tend to prefer the types of environments we ourselves consider undesirable."

Life Cycle

Most human reproduction takes place by internal fertilization via sexual intercourse, but they have developed crude technological means of assisted reproduction and are working on artificial reproduction. Notably, despite their bioengineering technology's shortcomings are nearly finished with a process they call "in-vitro gametogenesis" which would allow them to create gametes from any cell, thereby allowing even same sex couples to bear genetically related offspring.

The average gestation period for a human is 38 weeks, but a normal pregnancy can vary by up to 37 days. Embryonic development in the human covers the first eight weeks of development; at the beginning of the ninth week the embryo is termed a fetus. Humans are able to induce early labor or perform a cesarean section if the child needs to be born earlier for medical reasons.[193] In developed countries, infants are typically three to four kilograms (seven to nine pounds)3–4 kg (7–9 lb) in weight and forty seven to fifty three centimeters (nineteen to twenty one inches)47–53 cm (19–21 in) in height at birth. However, low birth weight is common in poorer human nationstates, as well as a key factor in the high levels of infant mortality in these regions.

Human childbirth is dangerous, with a much higher risk of complications and death compared to most species. This is due to several biological issues arising from their evolutionary history which could be trivially cured with medical technology readily available within our sector. Given human labor can last for a full day and is so painful their females are often given dangerously addictive drugs to block out the pain, using our ability to cure this ill as a bargaining chip would be exceptionally cruel.

Human children are helpless at birth (a rare trait in sapient species) and require constant and immediate care to survive. In a typical human family both the mother and the father provide care for their offspring, in contrast to other primates, where parental care is mostly done by the mother. Humans requite approximately fifteen to seventeen15-17 years to reach sexual maturity, but are not considered adults culturally until they reach eighteen18 years. Interestingly the human brian continues its growth and development stage until approximately twenty five25 years of age (The average sapient species reaches sexual and mental maturity simultaneously.). Humans are aware of this fact, yet refuse to change their cultural and legal definitions of adulthood to match their biological age of adulthood.

Age Based Degenerative Diseases

Humans suffer from several conditions which cause extreme mental decay in their later years. Dementia is the most common of these conditions. It involves the loss of cognitive functioning — thinking, remembering, and reasoning — to such an extent that it interferes with a human's daily life and activities. Dementia ranges in severity from the mildest stage, when it is just beginning to affect a person's functioning, to the most severe stage, when the person must depend completely on others for basic activities of daily living, such as feeding oneself.

One of the most feared causes of dementia is called Alzheimer’s Disease. Alzheimer's disease accounts for sixty to eighty percent60-80% of dementia cases. Alzheimer's is a progressive disease, where dementia symptoms gradually worsen over a number of years. In its early stages, memory loss is mild, but with late-stage Alzheimer's, individuals lose the ability to carry on a conversation and respond to their environment. On average, a person with Alzheimer's lives four to eight years after diagnosis but can live as long as twenty20 years, depending on other factors.

By galactic definitions and laws, the decay caused by these conditions is typically so severe that the individual cannot be classified as sapient in the later stages. There is no cure and treatments can only lengthen the time it takes for the decay to set in. Humans are extremely likely to spend significant time and effort attempting to cure this condition once given access to more advanced scientific knowledge.

Available medical and genetic technology could increase the human lifespan to approximately two hundred and forty four244 years, and delay the onset of middle and old age until the final four decades. Programs are already underway to be able to deliver life extension treatments to human generations starting with those born after nineteen eighty1980. These are intended to be given in exchange for assistance with several key problems effecting the sector.
